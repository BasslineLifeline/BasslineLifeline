Know The Signs

Overdose response, step by step.

If you suspect an overdose, every second matters. Here's exactly what to do.

01

Check for response

Shout their name, rub your knuckles hard on their sternum. No response is a red flag — slow or no breathing, blue lips, or gurgling sounds mean it's time to act.

02

Call 911

Texas's Good Samaritan law protects people who call for help during an overdose from drug possession charges. Always call.

03

Administer naloxone

Spray one dose into one nostril. It's safe even if you're not sure it's an opioid overdose — it won't hurt someone if you're wrong.

04

Rescue breaths & rescue position

If they're not breathing, give rescue breaths. If they are breathing, place them on their side to prevent choking.

05

Stay & re-dose if needed

If there's no response in 2–3 minutes, give a second dose. Stay with them until help arrives — naloxone wears off before some opioids do.

Narcan & Safety

How to use Narcan, and why it's safe.

Naloxone is one of the safest medications that exists. Here's everything you need to know before you ever need to use it.

How to use it

  1. Lay them on their back. Tilt the head back slightly.
  2. Peel back the package and remove the nasal spray device. Don't test or prime it — it's ready to go.
  3. Insert the nozzle into one nostril until your fingers touch the bottom of their nose.
  4. Press the plunger firmly to release the full dose. That's it — one spray, one full dose.
  5. Watch for breathing within 2–3 minutes. If there's no improvement, give a second dose in the other nostril.
  6. Stay with them until EMS arrives, even if they wake up and feel fine.

Why it's safe

  • No abuse potential. Naloxone has zero recreational value and produces no high.
  • It can't hurt someone who isn't on opioids. If you're not sure, give it anyway — there's no harm if you're wrong.
  • It's not addictive and cannot be used to get someone "high" or sedated.
  • Legal to carry and use in Texas. Good Samaritan laws protect people who use it and call for help.
  • No medical license required. Anyone can carry and administer naloxone.
  • Side effects are rare and far outweighed by the alternative. Some people wake up with withdrawal symptoms — uncomfortable, but never as serious as an overdose.
Good to know: Naloxone only reverses opioid overdoses (heroin, fentanyl, oxycodone, and similar). It does not reverse overdoses from stimulants, alcohol, or other substances — but it's always safe to give if you're unsure, and you should still call 911 either way.
